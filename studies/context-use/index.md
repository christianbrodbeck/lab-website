## Project Overview
This study examines how people understand speech in everyday listening situations, especially when there is background noise, like at a cocktail party. We are interested in how the brain uses context to support speech understanding across adulthood, from younger adults to older adults, including those who use hearing aids.

## What Happens in the Study

### Behavioural Tasks

We will measure your hearing and how well you understand speech when there is background noise. These tasks measure attention, memory, and speech understanding, and some of them will be similar to a visit to the audiologist.

<div style="text-align: center;">
  <img src="{{ "/images/context-use/behavioural-tasks.png" | relative_url }}" width="600">
</div>

### EEG Recording

You will listen to an audiobook while we measure your brain’s electrical activity using sensors placed on your head (a technique called EEG, or electroencephalography). This will allow us to measure how your brain responds to different speech sounds.

EEG allows researchers to measure natural brain activity using sensors placed on the scalp. It does not involve any stimulation or radiation.

<div style="text-align: center;">
  <img src="{{ "/images/context-use/EEG.png" | relative_url }}" width="600">
</div>

### MRI Recording

We will measure your brain activity with functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) while you listen to another audiobook. This allows us to see which parts of the brain are involved in speech with and without background noise.

fMRI measures changes in blood flow in the brain, helping us understand which brain networks support speech comprehension.

## Learn More

### What do we mean by “speech in noise”?

- These are conversations that take place in everyday situations, like restaurants or parties.  
- Background sounds can make it harder to understand what someone is saying.  
- The “cocktail party effect” describes how the brain can focus on one voice or sound even when there’s a lot of noise around.  

For more information, see [the Wikipedia page on the Cocktail Party Effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cocktail_party_effect).

---

### What are behavioural tests?

- Behavioural tests are tasks where participants listen, respond, or make decisions.  
- These tests help researchers understand speech perception, attention, memory, and response speed.  
- They are widely used in cognitive and neuroscience research.  

For more information, see [the Wikipedia page on Neuropsychological Testing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuropsychological_test).

---

### What are brain signals?

- Brain cells communicate with each other using small electrical signals.  
- EEG records large-scale patterns of this activity.  

For more information, see [the Wikipedia page on Electroencephalography (EEG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroencephalography).

---

### What is fMRI?

- fMRI (functional magnetic resonance imaging) measures changes in blood flow in the brain when doing a certain kind of task.  
- It helps researchers identify which brain regions are involved in tasks such as speech understanding.  
- fMRI does not involve radiation.  

For more information, see [the Wikipedia page on Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_magnetic_resonance_imaging).

---

### Are these methods safe?

EEG and behavioural tasks are non-invasive and commonly used in research. fMRI does not involve radiation. Participants are allowed to take breaks and stop at any time.
